CrossFit

If you haven’t been living under a rock, you have probably heard of CrossFit. Or if you’re like me, you think it’s a bunch of sweaty men lifting heavy weights. While I’m not completely wrong about this, I now know that CrossFit is so much more. Valued at $4 billion in 2015, CrossFit has grown a cult following, and I’m here to figure out why (2).

CrossFit Overview

Founded in 2000 by Greg Grossman, CrossFit is a workout focused on “general physical preparedness” for any obstacle life has to offer (1). CrossFit trains its clients by combining the basics of: gymnastics, weightlifting, and sprinting in a competitive, high-intensity environment (1). This allows specific weights, measurements, and times to be tracked for concrete goals.

Contrary to popular belief, CrossFit is not just a hard workout. CrossFit is a lifestyle built from exercise, nutrition, and community (3). With their “garage gym” aesthetic, CrossFit has over 15,000 affiliated gyms (or “boxes”) worldwide and continues to grow and offer certification courses in training, nutrition, and medicine (3). There are so many mixed opinions on their intense workouts, but with its cult following I couldn’t help but investigate: is CrossFit really sustainable and good for you?

The Workout, Science, and Misconceptions

Surprisingly, there are several research articles that discuss the effects of a CrossFit workout on the body. Many address a common misconception that CrossFit may put you at a higher risk for injury compared to other forms of exercise. According to a research paper published in PubMed in 2020, CrossFit presents similar injury prevalence to weightlifting and powerlifting, with the majority of injuries being in the shoulders and knees (4). Though this may seem concerning, the mean prevalence of injuries is only 35.3% out of 1000 hours of training (4). Another study from 2017 makes the comparison that about 74% of runners experience an injury per year, while about 19.4% of CrossFit participants are injured per year (5). But of course, anyone is advised to have previous injuries checked out before participating in any kind of intense exercise.

CrossFit participants also supposedly have about 7% lower body fat percentage than the average person and have more bone mass and lean muscle mass (2). I am wary at figures like this because of course anyone that exercises regularly will have a higher muscle mass and lower body fat percentage, but nonetheless it shows that CrossFit works.

In terms of actual skill, CrossFit cannot sharpen all 10 critical areas of athleticism: cardiovascular/respiratory endurance, stamina, strength, flexibility, power, balance, speed, coordination, agility, and accuracy (6). A study published in Sport Sciences for Health only verified that Crossfit strengthens 6 out of the 10 (6). So if you are looking to specifically train speed, coordination, agility, and accuracy, CrossFit may not be the workout for you (6). I’ve learned that CrossFit is “jack of all trades, master of none” workout.

Community Outreach

CrossFit doesn’t only offer workouts, they have built resources about proper nutrition and offer guidance with meal plans. In addition to that, they run kids programs, and a charitable organization that strives to improve fitness, education, and nutrition in surrounding communities (3). Gosh, what do they NOT do?!

Final Thoughts

With all of the information above, I’d put CrossFit at a 8/10 on the Lauren Scale (an arbitrary measurement of accessibility, sustainability, and overall benefits). The way the workout is set up it allows for participants to have a sense of accomplishment and satisfaction when they reach new weights, reps, and times. If you have weak knees, shoulders, or your back, I would advise against CrossFit. With any workout comes risk, and I believe that CrossFit’s risk is minimal to its overall benefits. Also if you are an athlete trying to improve agility, speed, or coordination, CrossFit also may not be for you, but for the average Gen Z NARP (non-athletic regular person) CrossFit is a great holistic workout.

Workouts aside, their focus on the holistic health experience with exercise, nutrition, and community really make it a stellar program. With over 15,000 gyms, you can probably find one anywhere, and with workouts from 5am to 6pm, there’s really no excuse not to go. Honestly, now I’m hooked and you may be seeing an update from me soon from my nearest Crossfit gym.
